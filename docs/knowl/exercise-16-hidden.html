Let \(T: \IR^4 \rightarrow \IR^3\) be the linear transformation given by the standard matrix \(\left[\begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 3 & 2 & -3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & -10 \\ 1 & 6 & -1 & 3 \end{array}\right]\text{.}\) \​begin{enumerate}[(a)] \item Explain why \(T\) is or is not injective. \item Explain why \(T\) is or is not surjective. \end{enumerate} \end{problem} \​begin{solution} Compute
\begin{equation*} \RREF\left[\begin{array}{cccc}1 & 3 & 2 & -3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & -10 \\ 1 & 6 & -1 & 3 \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 0 & 5 & -9 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right]. \end{equation*}
\​begin{enumerate}[(a)] \item Note that the third and fourth columns are non-pivot columns, which means \(\ker T\) contains infinitely many vectors, so \(T\) is not injective. \item Since there are only two pivots, the image (i.e. the span of the columns) is a 2-dimensional subspace (and thus does not equal \(\IR^3\)), so \(T\) is not surjective. \end{enumerate}
